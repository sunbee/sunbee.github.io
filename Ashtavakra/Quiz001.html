Ashtavakra Gita

A question about the nature of , after a dream about natural disaster and loss, led to King Janaka's seeking out Ashtavakra for knowledge.

Assessment:


King Janaka had a dream in which his kingdom was beset by natural disaster. He was scavenging for scraps of food and found a piece of roti. The custom at the time was that a householder would not sit down to eat until any hungry person in his presence was fed first.

Fearful of losing his sole scrap of food, Janaka hid the roti under his clothes and sought a lonely and desolate place to sit down and eat. Even then, an eagle swooped down upon him and snatched the roti just as he was about to eat. Desperate, he cried out loud in his dream and woke up to find himself in his royal court, surrounded by courtiers.

Being a thoughtful person, Janaka wondered whether he had woken up from a frightening dream into pleasant reality or escaped from harsh reality into a pleasant dream. His questioning nature led him to seek answers from wise men and women in his kingdom, ultimately leading him to Ashtavakra, the brahma-gyani, who had attained knowledge of the brahman, the ultimate reality.

The ensuing conversation between one in the prime of life thirsting to learn about reality, and one at the pinnacle of wisdom giving the knowledge, is Ashtavakra Gita, the song of reality. Unlike the Bhagvad Gita, where Krishna gave the highest knowledge to the seeker Arjuna in the heat of battle, this exchange transpired in a royal residence, in a peaceful, joyous atmosphere.
